\section{Introduction}
Digital signatures are fundamental cryptographic primitives, allowing a user to authenticate a message and guarantee its integrity. For decades,
most practical signature schemes have relied on classical hardness assumptions such as integer factorization or discrete logarithms. However,
these assumptions are known to be broken by quantum algorithms, which motivates the search for alternative constructions secure in the post-quantum setting.
Lattice-based cryptography has emerged as one of the most promising frameworks for post-quantum security, as its underlying problems are believed to remain
hard even for quantum computers. Beyond security, modern applications often require signatures to interact efficiently with zero-knowledge proof systems,
for instance in privacy-preserving protocols. Designing lattice-based signatures that remain compatible with such proof systems is therefore crucial.
A first attempt in this direction was the construction of Libert, Ling, Mouhartem et al. (\cite{LLM+16}), which proposes a lattice-based signature scheme with
efficient protocols. Although conceptually appealing, their construction suffers from extremely large proof sizes (several hundreds of megabytes) making it impractical.
The paper studied in this report (\cite{mainPaper}) revisits this problem and proposes a new lattice-based signature scheme specifically designed to interface smoothly
with recent zero-knowledge argument systems, while reducing the size of the produced proofs. In the following, we analyse the main ideas behind
this construction, highlight its improvements over previous work, and examine some of its technical components in more detail.

\section{General Paper Analysis}

The goal of the paper by Jeudy, Roux-Langlois and Sanders (\cite{mainPaper}) is the design of lattice-based signatures that work well with zero-knowledge proof systems, a property known as "signatures with efficient protocols" (SEP). This is crucial because privacy-preserving authentication systems — like anonymous credentials, group signatures, e-cash and so on — require proving knowledge of a signature on a hidden message and because the systems deployed up to now revealed to be vulnerable to quantum computers.

One of the prior lattice-based SEP constructions is Libert et al. (Asiacrypt 2016) (\cite{LLM+16}). In the paper, the authors found a way to make lattice-based signatures work with zero-knowledge proof systems. However, their proofs of knowledge reached hundreds of megabytes (the paper provides a fair and quantitative analysis of this limitation). All cited works appear in top-tier venues (Crypto, Eurocrypt, Asiacrypt, Journal of Cryptology), and the paper itself was published at Crypto 2023.

The paper studied in this report (\cite{mainPaper}) aims to fill in the gap caused by the problem of these previous works. In order to do this, they generate the signature and commitment parameters together. This one change makes a difference: fewer witnesses, smaller SIS bounds, and much better parameters.

The methodology builds on well-established tools — Micciancio-Peikert trapdoors (\cite{MP12}), Rényi divergence, and the Yang et al. (\cite{YangAZXYW19-0})/ LNP22 zero-knowledge frameworks — and is technically sound. The paper also identifies and corrects an error in the fast mode of Yang et al., which is a valuable independent contribution.

The results are amazing. The proof sizes go down from around 670 MB to below 650 KB in the module instantiation, a reduction of over three orders of magnitude.

The weaknesses are not fundamental in the sense that they arise from the methodology rather than from insurmountable barriers. The main practical weakness is the remaining proof size overhead relative to classical constructions, but it simply reflects the general current state of lattice-based zero-knowledge proofs. It is plausible that further advances in proof systems will reduce these sizes further, and the paper's modular design allows to directly benefit from such future improvements.

The paper has attracted follow-up citations in the lattice cryptography literature, with works using SEP framework from this paper as a part of their considerations, consistent with the authors' stated goal of providing a versatile building block. These works generally affirm the paper's contributions and have not identified fundamental flaws, though some note on further efficiency improvements that could be done improve their constructions as well.

This paper, presented at Crypto 2023, was the first one to provide a practical way to create anonymous credentials that can withstand quantum computers. It is likely to remain influential as long as lattice-based cryptography remains the dominant post-quantum paradigm.

\section{Paper Contributions}
\subsection{Large Sizes Problem}
As mentioned in the introduction, the signature scheme from Libert et al. \cite{LLM+16} suffers from large sized proofs (30 to 40 times larger). In this section, we study why
this previous scheme yields such enormous sizes. In \cite{LLM+16}, the signature scheme relies on a commitment $\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{D_0 r} + \mathbf{D_1 m}$. To
prove knowledge of a valid signature on a hidden message, the prover must demonstrate knowledge of the opening $(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{m})$ such that $\mathbf{c}$ is well formed.
More precisely, the verification algorithm operates on the binary decomposition of the commitment vector. In our case, $\mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^n$ so the
protocol requires decomposing each coefficient $c_i$ into $l = \lceil \log_2 q\rceil$ bits. \\
This design choice induces 2 main inefficiencies :
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Witness expansion : The witness size for the ZKAoK scales linearly with the number of bits to be proven. Instead of proving knowledge of $n$ elements
          in $Z_q^n$, the prover must prove knowledge of $n \cdot \log_2 q$ binary variables.
    \item Constraint proliferation : To ensure the decomposition is valid, the ZK protocol must enforce the binary constraint for every bit. In frameworks
          based on quadratic constraints, this requires adding $\mathcal{O}(n \log q)$ quadratic equations to the proof system. Furthermore, the security
          reduction in \cite{LLM+16} relies on embedding a trapdoor within the decomposition matrix. This introduces a norm growth factor $\propto \sqrt{n\log q}$
          in the extracted solution, forcing the scheme to adopt overly conservative parameters to maintain security margins.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Solution}
To mitigate this, we optimize the interactions between the commitment scheme implicitly used by privacy-enhancing protocols and the
signature scheme itself. Instead of generating the public parameters of these 2 components independently from one another, the authors propose a unified
generation, using a common matrix $\mathbf{A}$. This indeed reduces the size of the proofs since the size of the
public key is smaller and it allows the commitment randomness $\mathbf{r}$ to be algebraically absorbed into the signature vector $\mathbf{v}$, eliminating
the need for a separate proof of opening.
\section{Signature Scheme}
The signature scheme proposed by the paper is based on the one studied in \cite{LLM+16}, along with the improvements discussed in the previous section.

The proposed construction is a lattice-based signature scheme with efficient protocols, designed to minimize the witness size required for subsequent
zero-knowledge proofs. While it inherits the high-level goal of \cite{LLM+16} (signing committed messages), its internal mechanics differ fundamentally
to achieve the efficiency gains outlined in Section~3.2. The scheme relies on the hardness of the Short Integer Solution (SIS) problem over lattices and
utilizes the trapdoor sampling framework of Micciancio and Peikert \cite{MP12}.

\subsection{Key Generation and Parameters}
The scheme operates over a modulus $q$ and dimensions $n, m$. The key generation process (Algorithm 3.2 in
\cite{mainPaper}) produces a public key $\pk = (\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}, \mathbf{u})$ and a secret key $\sk = \mathbf{R}$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^{n \times m_1}$ is chosen uniformly at random. Crucially, in the context of signing
          hidden messages, $\mathbf{A}$ is derived from a public seed via a hash function (modeled as a Random Oracle) to ensure it is statistically random and independent of the trapdoor.
    \item $\mathbf{R} \in \{-1, 0, 1\}^{m_1 \times m_2}$ is a short random matrix serving as the \textit{trapdoor}.
    \item $\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{R} \pmod q$ is computed such that the relation between $\mathbf{A}$ and
          $\mathbf{B}$ is hidden by the shortness of $\mathbf{R}$.
    \item $\mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{Z}_q^n$ is a random syndrome defining the specific lattice coset for signatures.
\end{itemize}
The public parameters also include a gadget matrix $\mathbf{G}$ and a commitment key $\mathbf{D}$, which are used during
the signing and verification phases.

\subsection{Signing Committed Messages}
The core innovation lies in the signing algorithm (Algorithm 3.3). To sign a binary message $\mathbf{m}$ hidden inside a
commitment $\mathbf{c}$, the protocol proceeds as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Commitment:} The user computes a commitment to $\mathbf{m}$ using the public matrix $\mathbf{A}$:
          \begin{equation}
              \mathbf{c} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{D}\mathbf{m} \pmod q
          \end{equation}
          where $\mathbf{r}$ is a Gaussian randomness vector. Note that $\mathbf{A}$ is shared between the signature key and
          the commitment scheme.

    \item \textbf{Preimage Sampling:} The signer chooses a tag $\tau$ and uses the trapdoor $\mathbf{R}$ to sample a
          short vector $\mathbf{v}'$ satisfying a modified linear equation:
          \begin{equation}
              [\mathbf{A} \mid \tau\mathbf{G} - \mathbf{B}] \mathbf{v}' = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{c} \pmod q
          \end{equation}
          Substituting $\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{D}\mathbf{m}$ and $\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{R}$,
          the equation becomes:
          \begin{equation}
              \mathbf{A}\mathbf{v}'_1 + (\tau\mathbf{G} - \mathbf{A}\mathbf{R})\mathbf{v}'_2 = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{A}\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{D}\mathbf{m}
          \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Randomness Merging:} The final signature vector $\mathbf{v}$ is constructed by subtracting the commitment
          randomness $\mathbf{r}$ from the first part of $\mathbf{v}'$:
          \begin{equation}
              \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}' - \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{r} \\ \mathbf{0} \end{bmatrix}
          \end{equation}
          The resulting signature is the pair $\sig = (\tau, \mathbf{v})$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Verification}
Verification (Algorithm 3.4) checks the validity of the signature without knowing $\mathbf{r}$. The verifier computes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:correctness}
    [\mathbf{A} \mid \tau\mathbf{G} - \mathbf{B}] \mathbf{v} \stackrel{?}{=} \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{D}\mathbf{m} \pmod q
\end{equation}
By substituting $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}' - [\mathbf{r}^T \mid \mathbf{0}^T]^T$, the term $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{r}$ from the
commitment cancels out with the $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{r}$ implicitly contained in $\mathbf{v}'$. This algebraic cancellation
is the key mechanism: it allows the verifier to check the signature against the message $\mathbf{m}$ directly, without
needing to verify the commitment opening $\mathbf{r}$ separately. Additionally, the verifier checks that
$\|\mathbf{v}\|$ is sufficiently small to ensure security against lattice reduction attacks.

\subsection{Security Considerations}
The reuse of matrix $\mathbf{A}$ for both the signature trapdoor and the commitment introduces a potential vulnerability:
if the signer could choose $\mathbf{A}$ maliciously, they might break the hiding property of the commitment. To prevent
this, the scheme requires $\mathbf{A}$ to be generated via a hash function (Random Oracle Model) or a trusted setup,
ensuring $\mathbf{A}$ is statistically uniform and independent of the trapdoor $\mathbf{R}$. The security of the scheme
is proven under the SIS assumption, distinguishing between Type I forgeries (new tag) and Type II forgeries (reused tag),
with the latter requiring the use of Rényi divergence to handle the noise distribution introduced by the randomness merging.

\subsection{Protocol Description}

The e-voting protocol proceeds in four phases.

\textbf{Phase 1: Election Setup.} The election authority runs \textsf{KeyGen} (Algorithm~3.2) to generate its signing keys $(\pk, \sk)$. The public parameters $\textsf{pp}$ and the public key $\pk = (\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}, \mathbf{u})$ are published. The secret key $\sk = \mathbf{R}$ is stored securely.

\textbf{Phase 2: Voter Registration.} Each voter generates a secret key $\mathbf{s} \sample \{0,1\}^{m_s}$ via \textsf{UKeyGen} (Algorithm~5.4). The voter then interacts with the authority through the \textsf{Issue} protocol (Algorithm~5.5): the authority signs the message $\mathbf{m}_e = [\mathbf{s} \| \text{attributes}]$ via oblivious signing, producing a credential $\sig = (\tau, \mathbf{v})$. The tag $\tau = F(\textsf{st})$ is unique per voter, where $F$ is an injective function and $\textsf{st}$ is a counter incremented after each issuance.

\textbf{Phase 3: Vote Casting.} To cast a vote, the voter runs the \textsf{Show} protocol (Algorithm~5.6): they produce a zero-knowledge proof $\pi$ demonstrating that they possess a valid credential $(\tau, \mathbf{v})$ on some message $\mathbf{m}_e$ satisfying $\textsf{Verify}(\pk, \mathbf{m}_e, (\tau, \mathbf{v})) = 1$. The proof reveals \emph{only} the tag $\tau$ and the vote~--- the voter's identity, secret key, and other attributes remain hidden.

\textbf{Phase 4: Verification and Tallying.} The bulletin board accepts a ballot $(\tau, \text{vote}, \pi)$ if and only if (i)~the proof $\pi$ verifies, and (ii)~the tag $\tau$ has not been used before. If a voter attempts to vote twice, their tag will already appear on the board and the second ballot is rejected.


\subsection{Security Properties}

The system achieves the following security properties, directly inherited from the anonymous credentials construction:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
    \item \textbf{Anonymity.} The bulletin board reveals only $(\tau, \text{vote})$. The link between a voter's identity and their tag is protected by the zero-knowledge property of the proof system (LNP22 framework~\cite{LNP22}).
    \item \textbf{Unforgeability.} Creating a valid credential without the authority's secret key requires solving the SIS problem, which is believed to be hard even for quantum computers. The EUF-CMA security of the signature reduces to $\text{SIS}_{n, m_1, q, \beta}^{\infty,2}$ via the proofs in Lemmas~3.2 and~3.3 of~\cite{mainPaper}.
    \item \textbf{Double-vote prevention.} Each voter receives a unique tag $\tau$. Since tags are revealed on the bulletin board, any duplicate is immediately detected.
    \item \textbf{Post-quantum security.} The underlying Module-SIS and Module-LWE problems are not known to be solvable in polynomial time by quantum algorithms.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Key Implementation Details}

\textbf{Gadget matrix.} We construct $\mathbf{G} = \mathbf{I}_n \otimes \mathbf{g}$ using NumPy's \texttt{np.kron}, producing a matrix of size $n \times nk$ where $k = \lceil \log_2 q \rceil = 12$ for $q = 3329$.

\textbf{Gadget decomposition.} The \texttt{gadget\_solve} function decomposes an integer $t \bmod q$ into its base-2 representation, returning a vector $\mathbf{z} \in \{0,1\}^k$ such that $\mathbf{g}^T \mathbf{z} = t \bmod q$.

\textbf{Tag inversion.} The modular inverse $\tau^{-1} \bmod q$ is computed via Fermat's little theorem as $\tau^{q-2} \bmod q$, leveraging Python's built-in modular exponentiation.

\textbf{Randomness absorption.} The signature absorbs the commitment randomness by computing $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}' - [\mathbf{r}^T \mid \mathbf{0}]^T$, which algebraically cancels the $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{r}$ term during verification, as shown in Equation~\eqref{eq:correctness}.

\section{Conclusion}

In conclusion, the paper makes an important contribution to lattice-based cryptography by addressing a challenge that is both technical and practical: how to design a post-quantum signature scheme that remains efficient in privacy-preserving applications. Rather than making a simple optimization, the authors redesign the interaction between the signature and the commitment mechanism, significantly reducing the size of the zero-knowledge proofs required to prove possession of a valid signature on a hidden message.

What makes the construction particularly interesting is that these efficiency gains do not come at the cost of security. The scheme still relies on standard lattice assumptions, like SIS, while using trapdoor sampling and careful parameter design. The paper shows that efficiency in modern cryptography depends not only on the security of a primitive itself, but also on how well it interacts with other components such as commitments and zero-knowledge proofs. More generally, it shows that lattice-based constructions can be made much more practical for real-world privacy applications.

On the implementation side, we have reproduced the parameter estimates from Table~H.4, implemented the signature scheme (Algorithms~3.1--3.4) with a corrected \texttt{SampleD}, and built a complete e-voting simulation demonstrating the anonymous credentials construction. In the second half of the project, we plan to dive deeper into the security proofs (particularly the Type~I/Type~II forgery reductions and the role of the Rényi divergence), and to compile and benchmark the C~implementation from the \texttt{truncated-sampler} repository to obtain runtime performance figures complementing our size estimates.
